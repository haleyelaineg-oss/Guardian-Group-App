Cargo Securement Standards & Systems

Example standards for building an intentional securement practice. Adapt these to fit your operation

Standard 1: Equipment identification system

All chains, binders, and straps must be clearly identifiable by their Working Load Limit (WLL) before use. Equipment with ratings that have worn off or become illegible are tagged as out of service until replaced or re-labeled.

system: Color-coding by WLL Mark all binder handles and strap ratchet handles with colored tape or zip ties by rating:
Yellow:

5,000 lb WLL

Red:

10,000 lb WLL

Blue:

20,000 lb WLL

 Choose your colors and post the key on all trailer tongue boxes.
system: Dedicated rated kits - No mixing Each trailer carries a pre-assembled securement kit with matched components (chain + binder rated to the same WLL). Mixing ratings from different kits is not permitted without confirming the assembly WLL is appropriate for the load.
Standard 2: Pre-departure verification

The driver completes final verification. The driver may delegate securement tasks to crew members, but verification before departure belongs to the driver and should not be delegated.

do: Assign crew roles

Before every load, assign by name: who secures driver side, who secures passenger side, and who performs the final check. "You get driver side, you get passenger side, I'll walk it before we go."
do: Touch-and-pull final check

Before departing, driver physically pulls each tiedown. Any slack = tighten before moving.
don't: Rely on assumed responsibility

"Someone else loaded it" is not a valid defense. The driver is responsible for verifying securement before departure, even if they didn't load the cargo.

Standard 3: In-transit re-securement

Re-securement checks are scheduled events, not reactive ones. Waiting until something looks wrong is not a system. These intervals are federal requirements (49 CFR §392.9) — build them into the route.

do: First stop rule All crews stop and re-check securement within the first 50 miles of every trip. Loads settle and tension drops up to 30% as the rig moves. This stop is non-negotiable.
do: Re-check at every fuel or meal stop Any time the driver exits the cab for more than a brief stretch, tiedowns are checked before continuing. On long hauls, actively plan a stop every 150 miles or 3 hours.
do: Unusual event trigger Any hard brake, swerve, rough road, or near-miss triggers an immediate pull-over and check before continuing — no exceptions.
Standard 4: Equipment and tool maintenance

Worn, damaged, or unlabeled securement equipment is removed from service immediately. A crew making do with marginal equipment is a predictable failure waiting to happen. Build regular inspection into your maintenance calendar.

do: Out-of-service criteria Any chain, strap, or binder is pulled from service if: the WLL label is missing or illegible; any link is bent, cracked, or has more than 10% wear; a strap has cuts, fraying, or heat damage; a ratchet does not lock positively.
system: Trailer box standard Each trailer has a designated securement storage box. The kit contains only rated, labeled, serviceable equipment. Box is restocked after any job where equipment was used. Drivers sign off that the kit is complete at start of shift.
system: Edge protection kit Keep rubber edge protectors in every trailer box. Required by FMCSA when a strap contacts a sharp edge. Cheap to stock, expensive to skip.

What an intentional system vs. a habitual system looks like on the same crew

Habitual — how it often goes

"Whoever finishes first grabs the chains."
"I can't see the WLL on this one — should be fine, we've used it before."
Chains tightened once, never re-checked on the road.
"The loader always rides fine, we've done it a hundred times."
Nobody knows where the first aid kit is until it's needed.

Intentional — what the standard looks like

"Jake, you take driver side. Maria, passenger side. I'll walk it before we roll."
Strap is tagged yellow — 5K WLL. Loader is 8,400 lbs. Pull two more from the box.
Crew knows: first stop is within 50 miles. Pulls over, checks every tiedown.
Trailer is parked on level ground. Ramp angle checked before unloading.
Glove box card shows: first aid kit, driver side cab, behind the seat.
Implementing these standards on your crew — a practical starting point

Week 1: Inventory and label

  • Pull every chain, strap, and binder from every trailer
  • Remove anything without a readable WLL rating
  • Apply color code to all keepers by WLL
  • Post the color key inside each trailer tongue box

Week 2: Assign and communicate

  • Introduce the role assignment standard to all crews
  • Practice the 30-second role-assignment exchange
  • Fill out Vehicle Load Cards for each truck
  • Review first stop rule with every driver

Week 3+: Normalize and reinforce

  • Leaders do random walk-arounds before crews depart
  • Debrief any securement near-miss within 24 hours
  • Add securement kit check to vehicle pre-trip
  • Celebrate when the system works — not just when something goes wrong
